## Categorical & feature handling

This section covers how tri-boost turns raw rating factors into the binned, ordered axes that the oblivious split-finder consumes, and how those choices propagate into the granularity, semantics, and exactness of the exported 1D/2D/3D tables. The unifying theme: **everything here is upstream of the split search**, so almost all of it composes with depth-3 oblivious trees "natively" — the real battle is not oblivious-compatibility but (a) preserving the *raw-feature* provenance that keeps the ≤3rd-order fANOVA guarantee meaningful, and (b) keeping each table axis legible as a deployable rating dimension.

### The core categorical pipeline: ordered TS + empirical-Bayes shrinkage (the v1 spine)

tri-boost's categorical strategy should be a **single clean pipeline**: encode each categorical to *one ordered numeric axis* via a leakage-free, prior-shrunk target statistic, then let the symmetric histogram splitter treat it as any other numeric feature. This is exactly CatBoost's approach for oblivious trees — categoricals become numeric *before* the symmetric split, so the one-border-per-level rule and the 8-leaf table are untouched, and the fANOVA axis remains the (encoded) feature.

**The shrinkage is the part that matters more than the encoder family.** The decisive lever separating good from useless target encoding is shrinking each category's estimate toward the (exposure-weighted) global mean by an amount that grows as the category's count shrinks: `encoding(c) = (n_c·mean_c + m·global_mean)/(n_c + m)`. CatBoost writes the same idea as the prior `a·p` in the ordered-TS numerator. sklearn's `smooth='auto'` sets `m` by an empirical-Bayes estimate (`m ≈ within-var/between-var`) from the between- vs within-category variance, so well-separated categories shrink little and noisy ones shrink hard with **no tuning** ([sklearn TargetEncoder](https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/generated/sklearn.preprocessing.TargetEncoder.html); [Pargent et al. 2022](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2104.00629)). For insurance this is non-negotiable: a singleton postcode or a rare vehicle model *must* collapse to the base rate or the rating table is over-confident and unstable. **Ship empirical-Bayes auto-`m` as a v1 default — it is the new, cheap, tuning-free part — but note it is the *prior* component, complementary to and not a substitute for the leakage-avoidance mechanism.**

**Leakage avoidance is a separate problem** solved by *ordering* (CatBoost ordered TS) or *cross-fitting* (sklearn's out-of-fold scheme), and the spec must keep these two concerns distinct. Plain mean encoding `mean(y|c)` over all rows including row `i` is the documented anti-pattern: a singleton category memorizes `y_i` exactly, giving perfect train signal and zero generalization (the "prediction shift") ([scikit-learn TargetEncoder docs](https://scikit-learn.org/stable/modules/generated/sklearn.preprocessing.TargetEncoder.html)). tri-boost must **forbid raw mean encoding and ship a test that flags it**: encoded-feature importance must not spike on a shuffled target.

Two leakage-free routes, both worth offering:

- **Ordered TS (CatBoost-style, online).** Encode with only the examples *preceding* row `k` in a random permutation σ: `x̂_k = (Σ_{j: σ(j)<σ(k), x_j=x_k} y_j + a·p)/(Σ_{j: σ(j)<σ(k), x_j=x_k} 1 + a)` ([CatBoost cat-to-numeric](https://catboost.ai/en/docs/concepts/algorithm-main-stages_cat-to-numberic); [NeurIPS 2018 paper](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/1706.09516)). Because `y_k` never enters its own encoding, the train/test conditional distributions match. The detail beyond the basic formula: CatBoost resamples **s+1 permutations per tree** (σ₀ for leaf values, σ₁…σₛ for splits) to cut the *variance* of the early-rows-have-low-information artifact, and for regression first quantizes the target into `ctr_target_border_count` borders (default 1, i.e. binarized at the median on CPU).
- **K-fold cross-fitted encoding (batch).** Split train into K folds; encode each fold's rows using means from the *other* K−1 folds, then shrink ([sklearn TargetEncoder](https://scikit-learn.org/stable/auto_examples/preprocessing/plot_target_encoder.html)). This is the simpler, fully-batch, trivially-parallel alternative — no permutation machinery, deterministic given the fold seed — and has lower variance than the online "first rows are starved" artifact. It is a strict special case of ordered TS (disjoint hold-outs), so ship it as an easy deterministic baseline at v1.5 but treat ordered TS as the differentiator. **Do not take a runtime dependency on sklearn's `TargetEncoder`** — reimplement the trivial K-fold cross-fit internally so encoding is owned, seeded/deterministic, and serialized into the model artifact.

**Pricing adaptations that are mandatory, not optional.** For Poisson/Gamma/Tweedie with exposure offsets:
1. The shrink target and global mean must be the **exposure-weighted / link-appropriate rate** (frequency global mean = exposure-weighted base rate `Σ w·y / Σ w·e`), not a bare `mean(y|c)`.
2. For regression, **raise `ctr_target_border_count` well above the default 1** — median binarization of the target throws away most of the signal for continuous severity/pure-premium.
3. The empirical-Bayes variance estimate assumes Gaussian noise, so count/severity targets may need a deviance-aware or weighted variance form for `m`.

**Train/serve binning mismatch is the real footgun.** Train-time out-of-fold encodings (fold noise) differ from serve-time full-data encodings and can land a row in a *different histogram bin*. The model artifact must persist (a) the fixed inference-time category→encoding map and (b) the bin borders, and the encoder/binning must be re-applied identically at predict time. Always **export the category→encoding map alongside the tables** so the rating table reads on the original category labels, not on "TS-bin 0.37".

**fANOVA semantics: this is the one genuine wrinkle.** A category encoded via a *target-dependent* TS makes that feature's axis a learned function of `y` and the permutation. The decomposition is still **exact** for the model — leaves stay constant, the lookup-table form is piecewise-constant, order ≤3 holds (a correction to records that called this `changes_table_form`/`breaks_exactness`). What changes is *semantics*: the table axis is a target-statistic bin, which partially erodes the clean "category → relativity" reading actuaries want. The `Counter` (label-free) encoder is the safest on this axis (see below), and for any target-based encoder you should decide explicitly whether the "pure" effect is reported in transformed-feature space (where fANOVA is exact) or re-expressed back on the raw-category axis.

### Counter (frequency) encoding: the leakage-free freebie

`Counter` encodes a level by prevalence, `ctr = (curCount + prior)/(maxCount + 1)`, never touching `y` ([CatBoost cat-to-numeric](https://catboost.ai/en/docs/concepts/algorithm-main-stages_cat-to-numberic)). Because it is target-independent it needs **no permutation/ordering machinery** and is fully exactness-preserving for the single-column case. It is a robust prevalence signal — particularly stabilizing for near-ID columns where target stats are noisy — and in insurance a prevalence axis is exposure-like and often genuinely predictive. **Ship it at v1.5 as the safest categorical axis.** Two caveats: choose `counter_calc_method=SkipTest` (compute counts on train only) for clean separation; and a raw-prevalence feature risks learning portfolio composition or proxying a protected attribute, so keep it opt-in and constrainable. The exactness verdict is conditional on it being a *single-column* Counter — a Counter over a feature *combination* re-enters the >3-raw-feature danger below.

### The interaction-order landmine: feature combinations are `breaks_exactness`

This is the most important compatibility call in the section. CatBoost's greedy **categorical combinations** concatenate categoricals already used in the current tree with each remaining categorical and CTR-encode the cross (e.g. `city × product`), growing the lattice greedily from the second split onward, capped by `max_ctr_complexity` (default **4**) ([NeurIPS 2018 paper](https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/html/1706.09516); [CTR params](https://catboost.ai/en/docs/references/training-parameters/ctr)). The accuracy gain is real (the paper reports `c_max` 1→2 improving logloss ~1.86%).

But a single combination column packs a multi-feature interaction into **one axis that the tree treats as one feature**. A depth-3 oblivious tree splitting on a 2-categorical combination plus two other features now depends on **four** raw features — silently breaking the `≤3 raw features ⇒ exact ≤3rd-order fANOVA` invariant that is tri-boost's entire reason to exist. **This is `breaks_exactness` and the verdict is `skip`.** The only invariant-safe setting (`max_ctr_complexity=1`) degenerates combinations to plain single-categorical CTR, i.e. it removes the feature. tri-boost must either forbid auto-combinations or track raw-feature provenance so no tree's realized support exceeds three *raw* features.

The **constructive carve-out** (file as a research backlog item, *not* as this technique): let a single oblivious tree split on up to 3 *distinct categorical ordered-TS axes*. Categorical×categorical[×categorical] interactions then surface as **exact, purified 2D/3D tables over raw factors** — delivering the real interaction-accuracy lever entirely inside the order-3/lossless invariant, with no combination CTR, no opaque crossed axis, and no provenance bookkeeping. This is strictly better than combinations for the explainable-table product. Relatedly, `model_size_reg` ([docs](https://catboost.ai/docs/en/references/model-size-reg)) — which shrinks split scores in proportion to a combination's storage cost `s_new = s_old·(1+u/U)^M` — only bites once CTR combinations exist, so it is a v2 no-op for pure-numeric/single-axis categoricals.

### One-hot for low cardinality: native and exact, but watch the 3-slot budget

For tiny-cardinality fields (`one_hot_max_size`, CatBoost CPU default 2), skip CTR entirely and one-hot encode; each level becomes a 0/1 indicator with a single border at 0.5, an exact leakage-free representation ([categorical-features docs](https://catboost.ai/docs/en/features/categorical-features)). This is the **cleanest possible mapping for a binary rating factor** — an exact 1.000-reference relativity row. The verdict is `preserves_exactness` (correcting records that said `changes_table_form`): leaves stay constant.

Two hard implementation rules:
1. **Reassemble dummies onto one categorical axis at table-accumulation time.** Exposing each one-hot dummy as an independent fANOVA feature/axis (a) fragments the main effect across dummy-tables, (b) manufactures spurious within-categorical "interactions" over the structurally-empty all-ones cell, and (c) breaks product-marginal purification conditioning on zero-mass cells. With reassembly, the merged-grid accumulation + purification pipeline is unchanged and losslessness holds bit-for-bit.
2. **Respect the depth-3 budget.** A `k`-level field spreads across `k` binary axes, so a single original categorical can consume *all three* of a tree's split slots and still represent its levels incompletely. Default the one-hot threshold low (use it as the canonical encoding for **binary/cardinality-2** factors) and route higher-cardinality fields to ordered TS (one slot, multiple borders).

### The Fisher-equivalence trick: partition-quality categorical splits *without* bitset splits

LightGBM/XGBoost split a categorical *directly* into two level-subsets `{S, complement}`, finding the optimal partition in `O(k log k)` (not `2^{k-1}`) via Fisher (1958): build the per-category gradient/hessian histogram, sort categories by accumulated `sum_gradient/(sum_hessian + cat_smooth)`, and sweep the sorted order as if it were a numeric threshold — Fisher's theorem guarantees the optimal partition is a contiguous prefix of that order ([LightGBM Features](https://lightgbm.readthedocs.io/en/latest/Features.html); [XGBoost categorical](https://xgboost.readthedocs.io/en/stable/tutorials/categorical.html)). The knobs (`max_cat_threshold=32`, `cat_l2=10`, `cat_smooth=10`, `min_data_per_group=100`, `max_cat_to_onehot=4`) carry over as regularizers.

**The native bitset split itself is a poor oblivious fit** (`research`/skip): the node condition is a learned subset-of-levels *bitset*, not a single `(feature, threshold)` numeric test, so it cannot be the shared one-split-per-level of an oblivious tree; a per-tree learned grouping also makes the fANOVA axis a coarsened model-chosen binning rather than per-category rows. CatBoost itself avoids this for its oblivious trees, encoding to numeric instead.

**The bridge — and the strong v1.5 candidate — is the sorted-by-encoded-mean ordinal split.** Encode each category by its (regularized, leakage-free) target statistic, *order* categories by that encoding, and put a numeric border between consecutive categories. Because the optimal contiguous cut is reachable in that order, you recover partition-split quality while the split stays a **single numeric threshold** and each category remains a distinct, ordered, human-readable table row ([Fisher 1958](https://www.jstor.org/stable/2281952); comparative encoder study [arXiv:2401.09682](https://lightgbm.readthedocs.io/en/latest/Features.html)). This composes perfectly with the symmetric one-border-per-level rule.

**The precise caveat:** Fisher's contiguity theorem holds for the mean of the quantity the loss averages, which is `sum_gradient/sum_hessian` *recomputed per node per iteration* — and equals the static target mean *only for squared error*. For log-link Poisson/Gamma/Tweedie with exposure offsets, a single static target-mean axis is provably **not** the exact-optimal order. So there are two flavors: (a) a *static* TS-sorted axis (essentially the ordered-TS-as-numeric-feature already planned — simple, exact-table, slightly sub-optimal ordering for non-squared losses), and (b) an *exact* variant that re-sorts the categorical's histogram by `Sg/Sh` each level inside the per-level summed-gain search (more accurate, adds a sort in the hot loop and a per-tree category→bit map the LUT exporter must render as the ordered category list). Ship (a) at v1.5; treat (b) as the optimization.

### Native categorical splits for very high cardinality (XGBoost/LightGBM): research-only

XGBoost's `enable_categorical` (with `max_cat_to_onehot`, `max_cat_threshold`) does the same sort-then-partition and stores a category bitset on the split ([XGBoost categorical](https://xgboost.readthedocs.io/en/stable/tutorials/categorical.html)); LightGBM's many-vs-many is the same Fisher machinery. For a *shared* per-level set `S` across all `2^level` leaves, the level-aggregated optimum is a stronger constraint and the `O(k log k)` optimality argument no longer cleanly holds — nobody ships Fisher partition splits inside oblivious trees, so any such adaptation is unproven and must be benchmarked against ordered TS on real high-cardinality pricing factors (postcode, vehicle model) before commitment. **Exactness survives only if the split is stored as an explicit level-subset bitset and the export grid is the full level set**; if instead you pre-encode to a single numeric TS and split by threshold, Fisher's set-split is moot. The two strategies are mutually exclusive per feature — `≤3-feature exactness` is preserved either way, but the deployable artifact differs (category-set axis vs ordered-numeric axis), and the ordered-TS path keeps the existing monotone machinery, which is a strong point in its favor (XGBoost does not document monotone/interaction constraints for partition splits).

### Rare-level collapsing and the smoothing relationship

Merge categories below a count threshold into a single `other` level before encoding, so no table row is built from a handful of rows ([LightGBM `min_data_per_group`](https://lightgbm.readthedocs.io/en/latest/Parameters.html), though that is a *split-time* floor inside LightGBM's native Fisher splitter, not the same as encoder-side collapsing — cite the encoder practice, e.g. feature-engine/sklearn `min_frequency`, as the true precedent). It overlaps heavily with TS prior-smoothing on accuracy/robustness; its *distinct* value is **table-size/readability/memory and unseen-level handling**. Two rules: collapse **by count only** (target-independent) to preserve ordered-TS unbiasedness — never threshold on the encoded value; and fit the threshold on train and freeze the level-map for scoring. Note that collapsing changes the feature's marginal, hence the purification reference measure `w`, so purified effects shift slightly (the merged "rare" row carries the pooled marginal mass) — harmless to exactness, but flag it in export. v1.5, as a readability complement to the v1 smoothing.

### Feature hashing: lossless but unreadable — skip

`bucket = hash(category) mod B` bounds memory for unbounded/streaming cardinality and handles unseen levels with no dictionary ([VW / hashing trick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vowpal_Wabbit)). The buckets are numeric/binary axes, so it *composes* with oblivious splits and the decomposition stays lossless — but **collisions merge unrelated categories into one fANOVA row**, so the rating table no longer maps to original levels (an actuary cannot read "bucket 47"). Target encoding already bounds the axis to one numeric column *without* collisions, so it dominates hashing for tri-boost. Keep hashing only as a research fallback for pathological free-text-like IDs where even a dictionary is too large.

### Missing values: prefer the reserved/edge bin, expose "missing" as a table row

Three primary-source strategies have different *legibility* (not exactness) consequences:
- **Learned default direction** (XGBoost/LightGBM): run the gain sweep twice (missing→L, missing→R), store one bit per split. Missing rows fold into the low/high side with **no dedicated table cell** — still exact and order ≤3, but *missingness becomes invisible in the exported table* (you cannot read "what does NaN cost").
- **Reserved bin** (sklearn HGBT, index `n_bins−1`) / **`nan_mode` edge bin** (CatBoost: NaN treated as `<` all values (`Min`, default) or `>` all (`Max`); `Forbidden` errors): missing gets its own bin, which becomes an **explicit, auditable axis category** ([CatBoost missing-values](https://catboost.ai/en/docs/concepts/algorithm-missing-values-processing)).

For tri-boost the **reserved/edge bin is strictly preferable** — a dedicated "missing" row is exactly what an actuary needs, and it composes with the one-border-per-level rule (missing is just bin 0 or bin `k`). The verdict is `preserves_exactness` (correcting records that called the NaN slot a table-form change): a NaN bin keeps leaves constant.

Recommended design: give NaN a **reserved bin and let the sweep place it on either side** (sklearn behavior, strictly more expressive than CatBoost's hard-pin to Min/Max), exposing `nan_mode={Min,Max,Forbidden}` only as a compat convenience. Edge cases to make deterministic and tested:
- If a feature has **no NaN in training**, no separating split is learned, so a predict-time NaN simply inherits whatever leaf its reserved bin maps to (CatBoost issue #2850 documents the Max→Min fallback). Define this as "NaN follows its reserved-bin coordinate" and test it as a reproducibility invariant.
- **Monotonicity bound-propagation must exclude the missing bin** from the ordered-weight comparison, or a NaN cell outside the natural order will spuriously trigger a `w_L ≤ w_R` violation — handle missing as an *unordered* category for constraint purposes.
- The purification **reference measure must include the NaN coordinate's empirical mass** on each axis (Laplace smoothing keeps the possibly-empty NaN cell well-conditioned) so the missing row purifies correctly and zero-mean conditions still hold.

### Binning and quantization: this *is* the table granularity

Border placement is not a back-room detail — the union of borders sets **how many rows each numeric table has** and *where the cell boundaries fall*. Trained models are not bit-identical across `max_bin` settings, and for pricing this is a *feature*: coarser bins give more readable, more stable relativities. Exactness is unconditional on any chosen grid (any border set is lossless on its own grid), so the impact is granularity/resolution, never a break.

**Defaults and the v1 spine:**
- **Quantile (equal-count / `Median`) binning** for predictability and even row counts, with **quantile estimation on a ~200k-row subsample** ([LightGBM `bin_construct_sample_cnt`](https://lightgbm.readthedocs.io/en/latest/Parameters.html); sklearn `subsample`) to drop a full-data sort from setup at negligible accuracy cost. Subsampling is a *reproducibility* concern, not an exactness one: seed the RNG and **persist the chosen borders in the model artifact** (the borders are the axis of every exported rating table). Critically, **sample with replacement proportional to exposure/sample weight, then compute unweighted quantiles on that subsample** — sklearn's binning does exactly this (`"Sampling with replacement to implement frequency semantics for sample weights"`); naive unweighted sampling under exposure offsets yields wrong borders.
- **Midpoint borders for low-cardinality numerics:** when a feature has fewer distinct values than `max_bins`, place borders at midpoints of consecutive distinct values (`thresholds = mean of each adjacent sorted pair`) ([sklearn BinMapper](https://github.com/scikit-learn/scikit-learn/blob/main/sklearn/ensemble/_hist_gradient_boosting/binning.py)). This gives **exact splits and one table row per real value** — a high-value, easy v1 win for ordinal rating factors (age, no-claims years, vehicle group), where the table reads 1:1 with reality. It only fires when distinct-count ≤ `max_bins`, so it is not a substitute for choosing a sensible `max_bins`; and the reserved missing bin (`n_bins−1 = 255`) must be accounted for so a 255-distinct feature does not overflow into the missing slot (sklearn uses `max_bins = n_bins − 1` data bins precisely to reserve that slot — tri-boost should cap usable data bins at 255 if storing binned values as `uint8`).

**The border-objective menu (v1.5 accuracy/compactness knob).** CatBoost exposes six border types; the placement *objective* changes resolution: `Median`=equal object count (=quantile); `Uniform`=equal width; `UniformAndQuantiles`=half each; `GreedyLogSum`/`MaxLogSum` maximize `Σ log(weight)` within buckets (greedy is the cheap default, giving fine resolution in dense regions); `MinEntropy` minimizes `Σ weight·log(weight)` ([CatBoost quantization](https://catboost.ai/en/docs/concepts/quantization)). For pricing readability you may *want* coarse, business-interpretable borders, so a dense-region objective like `GreedyLogSum` can fight that goal — expose border type **and per-feature border count** (CatBoost-style `per_float_feature_quantization`) rather than hardcoding one. Two interactions to respect: border choice gates monotonic feasibility (too few candidate borders can wipe out all monotone-feasible splits — raise `max_bin` on constrained features per [XGBoost's own warning](https://xgboost.readthedocs.io/en/stable/tutorials/monotonic.html)); and Hessian-weighted borders are objective-dependent.

**Hessian-weighted (2nd-order) quantile sketch (research).** Place borders so each bin carries equal *hessian mass*: pick borders at evenly-spaced values of `r_k(z) = (Σ_{x<z} h_i)/(Σ h_i)` (XGBoost's ε-approximate weighted quantile sketch) ([XGBoost model docs](https://xgboost.readthedocs.io/en/stable/tutorials/model.html)). It is the principled binning for the 2nd-order objective and especially relevant because tri-boost's pricing Hessians are strongly non-constant (exposure-weighted Poisson, Gamma) — but it requires a streaming weighted-quantile sketch (the hardest binning) and, because the Hessian is score-dependent under log links, doing it "right" forces per-tree re-sketching and breaks bin-once. A **static variant** (sketch once on the init-round Hessian, freeze borders) preserves bin-once and exactness but its gain is small for log-link objectives (init Hessian is near-constant in `F`). Ship plain quantile first; benchmark the static variant before committing.

### Text and embedding ingestion: skip the subsystem, keep the lesson

CatBoost can ingest raw text (tokenizers → dictionaries → `feature_calcers` BoW/NaiveBayes/BM25) and dense embeddings (LDA/KNN calcers), emitting numeric columns the trees split on ([text-to-numeric](https://catboost.ai/en/docs/concepts/algorithm-main-stages_text-to-numeric)). Each emitted column is individually oblivious-compatible, and strictly the order-3 cap and losslessness *survive* (constant leaves, per-row-deterministic scalars), so the honest verdict is `changes_table_form`, not `breaks_exactness`. But **BoW explodes one text column into thousands of token-features**, yielding thousands of tiny tables over opaque token-presence indicators, and LDA/KNN axes are dense projections of an entire vector with no human meaning — both defeat the readable-rating-table mission. KNN additionally needs a stored training index at inference, conflicting with the "model is just lookup tables" deployment story. **Skip the subsystem (`skip`).**

The generalizable lesson worth a one-line note: **any externally-computed numeric featurizer composes natively with oblivious trees and preserves the exact ≤3rd-order decomposition**, because it lives entirely upstream of the split-finder. So tri-boost never needs to internalize an NLP/embedding stack — a user can supply any text- or embedding-derived numeric columns as ordinary features and keep every invariant. If such an external featurizer is target-dependent, it should carry the same ordered/holdout leakage protection that ordered TS provides — which the existing TS machinery already supplies.
